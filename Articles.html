The start of a relationship and when it settled down

(This article is written originally in Burmese. I translated it using Google Gemini. I would later translate on my own.)

(An exploratory essay on philosophy without references.)

Almost all human relationships start with excitement and dynamism, but as they settle down, disagreements begin to arise. These disagreements almost always lead to arguments and fights. Because this tends to follow a predictable pattern, it is commonly accepted that "real friends are made only after a fight." I see three reasons why this phenomenon occurs.

(1) Self-Control

When interacting with someone we are unfamiliar with, we mostly restrain ourselves, not openly expressing our desires and opinions. Similarly, the person we are interacting with is also controlling their mind, just like us. Most of the feelings we hold back are often self-serving. Since both parties restrain most of their ego-based desires, their egos do not clash, and every meeting tends to go smoothly.

However, once they become close, both parties tend to prioritize their own needs in their interactions, giving rise to many disagreements. What most people wonder at this point is why these disagreements happen, even though they feel they haven't done anything wrong.

(2) Understanding Others' Minds

When two strangers meet, after the usual greetings, they exchange views. They share thoughts and ideas under various topics. The thoughts and views that are expressed and exchanged, even if not complete (due to factor number (1) mentioned above), usually share a general concept. At this stage, they are understanding, even if their dispositions don't align or their ways of thinking are different. They tend to suppress the desire to object, so the capacity for understanding is quite high, and conversations between the two are often smooth. They are also tolerant of each other's potential habits and characteristics.

After becoming close, however, one side wants the other person to agree with and see things the way they do. If the other person doesn't see it that way, they want to object and argue. They gradually forget that differences in ways of thinking and in personal habits and characteristics are possible, and problems begin to arise between people whose views do not align.

(3) Protecting the Relationship

People engage in the first two actions because they do not want to damage the relationship between them. They carefully manage everything, starting from the smallest action or word, and take time to consider whether something is good or bad, appropriate or inappropriate. They are very careful that the relationship does not break down, tending to watch over it constantly, like cultivating a sapling.

But as they become closer, they tend to release their inhibitions more and more, and they even start competing over who values the relationship more. They misjudge the strength of the relationship and start to neglect small disagreements, neither resolving them nor apologizing.

So, should we continue to nurture the attitudes we had at the start of a relationship, even in the middle, the "settled" period? Or is this just wearing a "mask," concealing our true selves for the sake of others, and should be avoided? Is fighting a "social relationship test" because only without the "mask" can we find a friend who truly suits us?

The expected reactions

(This article is written originally in Burmese. I translated it using Google Gemini. I would later translate on my own.)

In human social interactions within a community, there are responses that people consciously or unconsciously expect to receive from one another. Although no one explicitly states these expectations, their absence can lead to friction in relationships. Conversely, the degree to which we can fulfill these unspoken expectations indicates how smoothly our own relationships are progressing. Dale Carnegie, in his book How to Win Friends and Influence People, states that "everything we do springs from a desire to be great." These small responses are the things that boost that desire to feel important.

For instance, when we meet a stranger, we subconsciously want some kind of response from them, such as a smile, a friendly greeting, or an offer of a seat. When they fail to offer any of these, we often feel a sense of awkwardness or discomfort, don't we? Because their lack of attention seems to crush our inner sense of self-importance, we become displeased and frustrated.

This is not only true when dealing with strangers but also among family members. Within a family, even if we simply do a household chore, we desire recognition from other family members. Since this recognition is often not given, we end up suppressing the other person's "sense of self-importance and respect." The words most commonly heard from mothers in Myanmar households, such as "I'm just like a slave," arise because we fail to soothe or respond to their household competence with words or actions.

So, what do I mean by these "responses"? I mean all the things we do back in return, such as smiles, words, gestures, and actions. This refers to things like showing consideration to someone who is expecting consideration, expressing thanks to someone expecting gratitude, or giving sincere praise when praise is due. Most people who have smooth and successful relationships are skilled at doing these things.

To be able to do this, however, is not easy: first, one must set aside their pride; second, one must understand the other person's feelings and be able to think for them; third, one must be able to restrain and swallow some of their own thoughts and opinions; and fourth, one must be able to change their own mindset. In short, it is about prioritizing others over oneself. However, this, in turn, seems to contradict the 21st century's philosophy of prioritizing the self.
